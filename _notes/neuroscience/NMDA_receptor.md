### NMDAR subunit composition
The NMDA receptors is a thetramer, meaning that it is composed of 4 subunits. 
Two of the subunits are always NR1, which contains the coagonist binding site.
One of the other subunit is always NR2.
In the adult rat hippocampus, NR2A and NR2B are the predominant subunits.
The CA1 synapses contain multiple subtype of NMDARs, including NR1/NR2A, NR1/NR2B and NR1/NR2A/NR2B.

It is likely that different NMDAR subtypes have different properties due to the different subunit composition.
In particular, they could differ in:
- ligands affinity
- activation/deactivation kinetics
- coupling with other molecules (extracellular or intracellular)

The NR2A/NR2B ratio of synaptic NMDARs in the hippocampus tends to increase during development.
In adult CA1 synapses, NR2A tends to be more abundant than NR2B (60/40 maybe?).

Anyway all of this because I wanted to say that: homosynaptic plasticity is by far best characterized for one specific synapse out of hundreds: the Schaffer collatera-CA1 synapses of hippocampus. These are glutamatergic synapses, mediated by AMPARs.

In many of the plasticity-inducing experiments that were performed, both LTP and LTD required the activation of NMDAR.
Of course, the question was immediately made: how does the activation of the same receptor leads to two opposite outcomes (potentiation and depression of the synapstic transmission strength)?
One answer is that the degree of NMDAR activation, and hence the level of postsynaptic calcium elevation, dictates the direction of NMDA-dependent synaptic modification.
Among the evidence for this answer we have:
 - the conversion of LTP to LTD by a partial blockade of NMDARs.

Another answer could be that distrinct NMDAR groups, with distinct "preferred" activation conditions, leads to distinct direction of synaptic modification.
Among the evidence for this answer we have:
 - the experiments from Liu et al., 2004 (1400 citations)
